ABSTRACT



Nowadays, most of our daily lives are spent either on our computers or on our smartphones. These relatively new objects have become an extension of ourselves, and life without them seems unimaginable. This is a development of the past few years. Recent technological developments have increased the need to innovate, refresh and update. We want more, faster, better. Rapid digital advancements and new digital products create a huge amount of complex new spaces. To function within those spaces we need clear guidance, in the shape of Graphical User Interfaces (GUI). The more time we spend on our phones and computers, the more we see the world via the different interfaces of every (new) program or product that is introduced. Icons and symbols have taken on an important role in understanding our daily digital surroundings.

Although an image says more that a thousand words, whether or not digital interface signs really help us understand our daily digital spaces is something worth researching. There are already a lot of icons that result in miscommunication between human and computer. When the younger generation sees a floppy disk (the object) as a 3D-printed save button, it is evident that icons based on physical objects can be troublesome and/or inconvenient. Formats are revamped and replaced so often that they seem to disappear before they even exist. Designing an icon based on the physical world can be challenging in the fast-paced world we currently
live in.

In my thesis, I explore to what extent the icons and symbols that are meant to guide us are influenced by the physical world and if they also have an effect on the real world in return. Is there an interaction? And does this also count for symbols? This thesis is a research into the kinds of digital signs there are and what kind of styles are used designing them. As a graphic designer I often find myself in the digital realm and, although it is not new to me anymore, I still find icons that were probably based on real objects but whose original function is unfamiliar to me. Apart from spending a lot of time on computers, current graphic designers are experiencing a shift to the digital, designing more for the screen. Having the basic knowledge of how meaning is created and interpreted between screen and physical world is essential. In this thesis I question how signs on screen communicate with the user and how the design can influence this communication. This leads to the research question:

In what way does the design of interface symbols and icons affect the way we see the (physical) world, and, considering the future, should we re-think their design accordingly?

The main focus of this thesis will be 1) how meaning is created for icons and symbols on the screen, 2) how they can lose their meaning due to passage of time or rapid technological developments, and 3) when we should replace, redesign or get rid of them. The life and death of digital signs and when to kill them.
